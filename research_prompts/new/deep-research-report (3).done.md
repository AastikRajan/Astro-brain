# Shadbala Components and Formulas (in Virupas/Shashtiamsas)

**Kala Bala (Temporal Strength):** Kala Bala has seven parts. The **Nathonnātha Bala** (day‑night strength) is computed from the birth time relative to midnight.  Parāśara (BPHS 27) gives: let *Unnata* = “apparent birth time minus midnight” (in ghaṭīs, where 30 ghaṭīs = 12 hours).  Then *Nata* = 30 – *Unnata*.  For **Moon, Mars, Saturn** (night‑strength planets), Nathonnatha = 2×(*Nata*).  For **Sun, Jupiter, Venus** (day‑strength), Nathonnatha = 60 – *Nata*.  Mercury always gets full 60.  (In other words, each planet’s Nathonnatha goes from 60 at peak (noon or midnight) down linearly by 1 point per 24 minutes away from that peak【12†L112-L117】.)  

**Pakṣa Bala (Lunar Phase):** Let Δ = Moon longitude – Sun longitude (normalized to 0–360°).  Convert Δ to degrees mod 360.  Then Paksha Bala (in virupas) = (Δ/3).  If Δ > 180° (i.e. >6 signs), replace Δ by 360°–Δ (so the value stays ≤ 60).  This value (max 60) is the Paksha Bala **for benefics**.  For malefics, the planet’s Paksha Bala = 60 minus that amount.  (Parāśara: “Deduct Moon’s longitude from Sun’s; if >6 signs, subtract from 12; convert to degrees and divide by 3 to get benefic Paksha Bala; malefics get 60 minus that amount”【12†L121-L125】.)  Thus birth near Full Moon (Δ≈180°, Paksha ≈60) gives high Paksha Bala to benefics and low to malefics; near New Moon (Δ≈0 or 360°, Paksha≈0) the reverse.  

**Tribhāga Bala (Third‑part strength):** Divide day and night each into three equal segments.  Parāśara prescribes that one planet rules each third: Mercury rules the **first third of day**, Sun the **second third of day**, Saturn the **last third of day**; Moon rules the **first third of night**, Venus the **second third of night**, Mars the **last third of night**.  Jupiter always has full Tribhaga (60 virupas) at any time.  The planet ruling the segment of birth gets full 60; all others get 0 from Tribhaga.  (Thus there are always two planets with 60 – the hora lord (below) and Jupiter – others zero【12†L129-L135】.)  In Shashtiamsas this means 60 Shashtiamsas (1 Rupa) for the ruler of that third and 0 for others.  

- **Tribhāga assignment:**  
  - Day (sunrise to sunset): 1st-third = Mercury, 2nd-third = Sun, 3rd-third = Saturn.  
  - Night (sunset to next sunrise): 1st-third = Moon, 2nd-third = Venus, 3rd-third = Mars.  
  - **Always:** Jupiter = 60 virupas.  

**Varṣa/Māsa/Dina/Hora Bala (Year/Month/Day/Hour lords):** The **Varṣa-lord** (year‑lord) at birth gets 15 virupas; **Māsa-lord** (month lord) 30; **Dina-lord** (weekday lord) 45; **Hora-lord** (planetary hour lord) 60.  In practice one finds the weekday lord on the day Sun enters Aries (Varṣa), the sign‑entry weekday lord (Māsa), the weekday of birth (Dina), and the ruling planet of the hour of birth (Hora) and assign them 15, 30, 45, 60 respectively【12†L137-L139】.  In Shashtiamsas these are 15, 30, 45, 60 virupas.  

**Āyāna Bala (Declination strength):** Āyāna Bala depends on a planet’s declination (krānti) relative to the equator.  Using the Sun’s obliquity ε ≈ 23°27′ (23.45°):  
\[ \text{Āyana Bala} = 30 \times \frac{(\epsilon \pm \phi)}{\epsilon} = 1.2793\times(\epsilon \pm \phi),\]  
where φ = planet’s tropical latitude or declination (positive north, negative south).  The “+” or “–” sign is chosen per planet: *Moon & Saturn* add for southern (negative φ) and subtract for northern (positive φ); *Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Venus* add for northern φ and subtract for southern; *Mercury* adds for either (i.e. always uses ε+|φ|).  This yields 0–60 virupas, with maxima of 60 at ±23°27′ as appropriate【42†L94-L102】.  (Equivalently, planets in the “strong” hemisphere gain Ayana strength: Moon/Saturn stronger in south, Sun/Mars/Jupiter/Venus stronger in north.)  

**Yuddha Bala (Planetary War):** If two planets (Mars–Saturn) lie within 1° orb (“at war”), the one with the higher longitude wins.  The **difference of their total Shadbala** (in virupas) is **transferred**: add that difference to the victor and subtract from the loser【13†L178-L181】.  In other words, the winner gains `(Strength_winner – Strength_loser)` and the loser loses the same amount.  (Note: only Mars–Saturn range, and the war adjustment uses the overall Shadbala Pindas or totals.)  

## Sthāna Bala – Ucchā Bala (Positional/Exaltation Strength)

Ucchā Bala measures distance from debilitation.  A planet in exact exaltation has 60 virupas; in exact debilitation 0.  Parāśara gives: subtract the planet’s **debilitation degree** from its longitude.  If this difference > 180° (6 signs), subtract it from 360° again (i.e. take the smaller arc).  Then convert that distance into degrees and divide by 3.  The quotient (in virupas) is the planet’s Uchchā Bala【12†L71-L76】.  (This is a linear formula: at debilitation distance=0 gives 0 virupas; at exaltation distance=180° gives 60.)  

## Drik (Aspectual) Bala

Each classical aspect contributes a fixed strength: an **opposition (7th)** yields full strength (60 virupas) of Drik Bala; Mars’s special aspects (4th and 8th) give ¾ of full (45 virupas); Jupiter’s (5th and 9th) give ½ (30 virupas); Saturn’s (3rd and 10th) give ¼ (15 virupas).  (All planets have the 7th aspect; only Mars–Saturn have these additional ones.)  In practice, if planet A aspects planet B at one of those special angles, B gains that many virupas from A’s aspect.  For example, any benefic aspect at opposition adds 60, a Mars aspect at a 4th/8th angle adds 45, etc.  【41†L147-L149】.  (For malefic aspects one might subtract similarly, but classical Shadbala uses these positive values for beneficial aspects.)  

## Minimum Strength and Interpretation (Iṣṭa/Kāṣṭa)

Classical BPHS gives minimum Shadbala **requirements** (total across all six Balas) for a planet to be considered strong.  These thresholds (in virupas) are: **Sun 390, Moon 360, Mars 300, Mercury 420, Jupiter 390, Venus 330, Saturn 300【41†L157-L160】.**  If a planet’s total Shadbala meets or exceeds its requirement it is deemed “strong”, and if it greatly exceeds it “very strong”.  

In practice modern texts interpret totals roughly as:  
- **>450 virupas** (≥7.5 R) = very strong,  
- **375–450** (6.25–7.5 R) = strong,  
- **300–375** (5–6.25 R) = average,  
- **<300** (<5 R) = weak【36†L119-L122】.  

The **Shadbala ratio** (actual strength divided by requirement) is often used as a quick gauge.  A ratio **≈1.0** means it just meets its classical need; below ~0.8 (i.e. <240 V for a 300 requirement) is considered weak; above ~1.2 (e.g. >360V on 300 req.) strong; very strong might be >1.5.  In other words, totals roughly 5–6 Rupas (~300–375V) are average, while >6.25 Rupas (375V+) are strong, consistent with classical thresholds【36†L119-L122】【41†L157-L160】.
