# Tithi Pravesh (Tithi‐Based Solar Return)

The **Tithi Pravesh** chart is cast at the moment each year when the Sun re‐enters its natal sign *and* the Moon–Sun angular separation (Tithi) equals the natal value.  In practice one computes the natal Sun–Moon angle (the birth Tithi) and the natal Sun’s Rashi (sign).  Then, around the time the Sun crosses that Rashi each year, one finds the instant when `(MoonLongitude – SunLongitude)≡(MoonLongitude_birth – SunLongitude_birth) (mod 360°)`.  This simultaneous condition – Sun in natal sign and Moon–Sun angle equal to natal – defines the Tithi Pravesh moment【7†L169-L178】【14†L31-L39】.  For example, if at birth the Sun was in Aries at 10° and the Moon 60° ahead (30° apart), then the next year’s Tithi Pravesh occurs when the Sun is again in Aries *and* the Moon is 30° ahead of it【7†L169-L178】.  Algorithmically, one typically:  
- Determine the natal Sun’s sidereal Rashi (R) and natal Tithi angle A = (Moon–Sun).  
- For each solar year, find when Sun enters R, then solve for the time when `(Moon–Sun) = A`.  
- The chart cast for that moment (at the birth place) is the Tithi Pravesh chart.  

This contrasts with the standard Tajika Varshaphala (annual solar return), which uses the Sun’s return to *exact natal longitude* (sidereal), ignoring the Moon.  In other words, Varshaphala marks the *solar year* (Sun back to natal posn) whereas Tithi Pravesh marks the *luni-solar year* (Moon–Sun alignment)【7†L169-L178】【20†L1-L8】.  (One source notes “Annual Tithi Pravesha chart is cast every year when Moon is exactly at the same angle from Sun as at birth, in the same soli-lunar month, with Sun in the natal Sun sign”【20†L1-L8】.)  

**Interpretation:** Tithi Pravesh uses Tajika methods (Hora‐chart analysis) but with its own emphasis.  The **Year‐Lord** is taken as the weekday-lord on the pravesha day (using sunrise‐to‐next-sunrise convention)【14†L45-L52】.  For example, if Tithi Pravesh occurs on Tuesday, Mars is Year-Lord; if on Friday, Venus, etc.  One then assesses whether that Lord is functional benefic/malefic for the natal lagna.  Another rule is to compare the Pravesh chart Lagna to the natal Lagna: if the pravesh Lagna is a Kendra (4th/7th/10th) from natal Lagna, the year tends to be successful; if a Trikona (5th/9th), prosperous; if a dusthana (6th/8th), problematic【14†L66-L73】.  Also the **Hora‐lord** (Sun‐ or Moon‐hour ruler at pravesh time) is noted as indicating the focus of events【14†L73-L81】.  In short: weekday‐lord = Year‐lord【14†L45-L52】; strength/placement of Year‐lord yields annual fortune; assess Lagna’s relation to natal Lagna【14†L66-L73】; and the Hora‐lord’s house and yogas indicate key themes【14†L73-L81】.  (One author summarizes: “Weekday at commencement… becomes lord of year”【14†L45-L52】.)  

**Year‐Lord determination:**  By convention the day-count starts at sunrise.  The weekday on which the Tithi‐Pravesh moment falls (using sunrise-adjustment) is taken as the Year‐Lord.  For example, if Tithi Pravesh occurs on a Saturday (Sunrise–Sunrise cycle), Saturn is the Year-Lord.  This planet’s condition (sign, house, aspects in the Pravesh chart) is then taken as significant for the year【14†L45-L52】.  

**Interpretation rules:**  Aside from Year‐Lord, astrologers note that Tithi itself is often said to influence relationships (since it ties to lunar phase) – but concretely one reads the Pravesh chart in Tajika fashion.  Thus a Pravesh chart with benefics in kendras relative to natal lagna is auspicious, etc【14†L66-L73】.  The Hora‐lord (Sun/Moon hourly ruler) at pravesh may also be noted – if Sun‐hora or Moon‐hora.  In summary, main rules are: use the Pravesh (annual) chart as a predictive chart, with Year‐Lord (weekday) as chief significator, plus analysis of Lagna placement and Hora-lord【14†L45-L52】【14†L66-L73】.  

**Differences from Varshaphala:**  Varshaphala (solar return) uses solar longitude only; Tithi‐Pravesh uses luni‐solar (tithi) alignment with Sun’s natal sign.  The Year-Lord convention differs as above (weekday vs sign-lord of solar return).  Interpretively, Tithi‐Pravesh is often used specifically for relationships and events linked to lunar themes, whereas Varshaphala is broader.  

# Pancha‐Pakshi Shastra (Five Birds System)

In Pancha‐Pakshi Shastra (a Tamil siddha timing system), every person is assigned **one of five birds** based on birth data.  The five birds are *Vulture (Shakuni)*, *Owl (Uluka)*, *Crow (Kaka)*, *Cock (Kirata)*, and *Peacock (Mayil)*【33†L108-L116】.  The assignment (“birth Pakshi”) depends on the **nakshatra** of the Moon at birth and the **Paksha** (waxing/shukla or waning/krishna) of that lunar day【29†L121-L129】.  A typical rule (from one source) is: 

- In **Shukla Paksha**:  
  - Nakshatras Ashvini–Mrigashirsha ⇒ **Vulture**; Ardra–Purvaphalguni ⇒ **Owl**; Uttaraphalguni–Vishakha ⇒ **Crow**; Anuradha–Uttarashada ⇒ **Cock**; Shravana–Revati ⇒ **Peacock**【31†L177-L185】【31†L189-L194】.  
- In **Krishna Paksha**:  
  - Ashvini–Mrigashirsha ⇒ **Peacock**; Ardra–Purvaphalguni ⇒ **Cock**; Uttaraphalguni–Vishakha ⇒ **Crow**; Anuradha–Uttarashada ⇒ **Owl**; Shravana–Revati ⇒ **Vulture**【31†L177-L185】【31†L189-L194】.

These correspond to the table in [31]: e.g. Ashvini–Mrigashirsha give Vulture (Shukla) vs Peacock (Krishna)【31†L177-L185】, Ardra–Purvaphalguni give Owl (Shukla) vs Cock (Krishna), etc.  (The ClickAstro article explicitly says “based on the birth star (nakshatra) and Paksha, find your bird from the chart below”【31†L177-L185】.)  

**Bird Activities:** Each of the five birds cycles through five **activities** at any given time: **Ruling, Eating, Walking, Sleeping, Dying**【33†L117-L124】.  Ruling (“Kaara”) is most auspicious, dying (“Marana”) most inauspicious.  (In Pancha‐Pakshi lore, “birds are most powerful when they rule and least when they die”【33†L117-L124】.)  Thus when your birth-bird is “ruling,” the time is strongly favorable; if it is “eating,” fairly good; if “walking,” neutral; if “sleeping” or “dying,” inauspicious for new endeavors.  

**Daily Cycle Algorithm:** Daytime (sunrise to sunset) and nighttime (sunset to next sunrise) each consist of **5 equal segments** (2h24m each, since 12h=30 ghatika; 30/5=6 ghatikas).  The sequence of activities in each segment depends on Paksha.  According to classical Pancha‐Pakshi tables【34†L418-L426】【34†L424-L430】: 

- **Shukla Paksha (waxing):**  
  - *Daytime (sunrise)*: Food → Walk → Rule → Sleep → Death【34†L418-L422】 (in order)  
  - *Nighttime (sunset)*: Food → Rule → Death → Walk → Sleep【34†L424-L430】.  
- **Krishna Paksha (waning):**  
  - *Daytime:* Food → Death → Sleep → Rule → Walk【34†L418-L422】.  
  - *Nighttime:* Food → Sleep → Walk → Death → Rule【34†L424-L430】.  

(Each arrow above indicates the successive 2h24m segment.  For example, in Sukla‐day, the first 2h24m after sunrise all birds are in “Eating,” next 2h24m all in “Walking,” then “Ruling,” “Sleeping,” and “Dying”【34†L418-L422】.  Night follows its own order【34†L424-L430】.)  

**Determining a Bird’s Activity:** To find what any bird is doing at a given clock time:  
1. Check if the Moon’s Paksha at that time is Shukla or Krishna.  
2. Compute local sunrise and sunset; determine if the time falls in daytime or nighttime.  
3. Find how many minutes have elapsed since sunrise (or since sunset). Divide by 144 (2h24m = 144 min) to get the segment index (0–4).  
4. From the sequences above, index 0→“Food”, 1→“Walk” (or “Rule” in Krishna day), etc.  
Thus you know *your birth-bird*’s current activity.  (All birds undergo the same cycle but offset by their “apahara” assignments.)  

**Predictions and Muhurta:**  Pancha‐Pakshi is used primarily for timing.  One selects times when the native’s main bird is engaged in *Ruling* (or at least Eating) to undertake important tasks【33†L117-L124】.  Conversely, when the bird is *Sleeping* or *Dying*, activities are considered inauspicious.  Also one may consider sub‐birds: e.g. the table [33] notes certain birds “rule” on specific weekdays, etc.  In effect, the chart of which bird is ruling/eating vs dying at any moment tells you auspicious vs unlucky intervals.  One source notes that if the main bird or its allied bird is ruling at a moment, that time is “beneficial and lucky”【33†L98-L104】, whereas if the main bird is dying, one should avoid it.  

# Lal Kitab System

Lal Kitab (the “Red Book”) contains a rich set of predictive rules distinct from classical Parashara.  Key elements include:

- **Planet‐in‐House Effects:**  Each of the 9 planets (Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn, Rahu, Ketu) has a specific aphorism for placement in each of the 12 houses.  The wording often differs from BPHS.  For example, *Sun in 1st house* is very auspicious – native gains courage, success and government favor【48†L5228-L5236】; *Sun in 5th* confers great intelligence and unexpected wealth【48†L5361-L5370】.  *Mercury in 1st* brings progress and profit in business【51†L6263-L6272】. *Jupiter in 1st* makes one a “teacher of the king,” shouldering responsibility with help from relatives and enjoying wealth【52†L6641-L6649】.  (Each planet’s effects in every house are codified in Lal Kitab verses; the full set runs 9×12=108 combinations.)  **Unlike BPHS**, Lal Kitab readings often emphasize social/material themes (e.g. Saturn yields poverty, etc.) and give prescriptive remedies.  (See [48],[51],[52] for sample entries.)  

- **Sleeping (“Dormant”) Planets:**  Lal Kitab introduces the concept of *sleeping* (dormant) planets.  A planet is **dormant** if the 7th house from it is empty.  For example, if Jupiter is in the 1st and the 7th house (Cancer) has no planet, “Jupiter will be called dormant (sleeping)” – its effects lie latent and often only manifest after marriage【54†L439-L447】.  Dormant planets/house can be “awakened” by various means (prayer, worship of certain deities, or by treating related planets).  Traditional texts give a table of which planet to propitiate to awaken each house【54†L453-L462】 (e.g. to awaken house 1, worship Mars; house 9, worship Jupiter; etc.).  

- **Karmic Debts (Rin):**  Lal Kitab identifies three main types of “Rin” (karmic debts) – **Pitru Rin** (ancestral debt), **Stree Rin** (women’s debt), and **Swa Rin** (self or “matru” debt).  Each has specific chart signatures.  For instance, Pitru Rin is indicated by affliction of the 9th (Guru) sector – e.g. damage to Guru’s house or Jupiter afflicted by enemy planets【60†L108-L113】.  Swa Rin is indicated by *Venus in the 5th house*【60†L143-L149】.  Matru (self) Rin is indicated by *Ketu in the 4th house*【60†L163-L171】.  Stree Rin is indicated if *Sun, Rahu or Ketu occupy the 2nd house*【60†L180-L188】.  (Lal Kitab claims these placements correlate with past-life debts affecting one’s life.)  The texts then prescribe specific remedies (feeding cows, charity, etc.) to “pay off” the debt.  

- **Annual (Varshkundli) Chart:**  Lal Kitab’s approach to the annual chart is quite different.  Instead of a solar-return chart, Lal Kitab uses a **progressive year‐chart** where each planet moves one house per year in a fixed sequence.  In effect, at age 1 the natal planets start in certain houses, then at age 2 each planet shifts one house forward, and so on cyclically【62†L25-L33】.  (A known Lal Kitab guide lists for ages 1–20 the house each planet occupies each year.)  Thus Lal Kitab Varsh‐kundli is constructed by applying this deterministic “Varshphal” sequence each year【62†L25-L33】, rather than waiting for the Sun’s return.  This yields an annual predictive chart where, for example, at age *n* one looks up where each planet has moved.  This method stems from a concept that “each planet spends one year in each house and then moves to the next house the following year”【62†L25-L33】.  

All of the above are *diagnostic* rules.  Lal Kitab’s famous remedies (donations, rituals) are omitted here; instead, one would compute these conditions and read off the stipulated effects.
